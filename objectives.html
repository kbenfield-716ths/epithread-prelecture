Pre-Lecture Module — Foundations of Medicine

What this module teaches

This session uses the Titanic survival dataset as an entry point to epidemiological thinking. The goal is not methods — it is the reasoning underneath them. By the end you should be able to look at a table of counts and ask better questions about it than you could before you started.

Through-line
Everything in this course sits somewhere on the arc from data → knowledge → wisdom. Data is counts and measurements. Knowledge is what we can reliably infer from them. Wisdom is knowing when our inferences hold and when the data misleads us. This module establishes that arc before you have any methods vocabulary — because the vocabulary only makes sense once you've felt the problem it solves.
Epidemiology Foundations Primary
  • 1 Define epidemiology in your own words — arrived at through data exploration, not received as a definition.
  • 2 Explain how population-level patterns inform individual clinical decisions — and where that reasoning breaks down.
  • 3 Explain why climate change is a public health concern and how epidemiology illuminates the relationship between environmental exposures and health outcomes. (The same causal reasoning you practiced here applies directly to wildfire smoke, extreme heat, and vector-borne disease.)
Association, Confounding & Causal Inference Primary
  • 4 Distinguish between an association in data and a causal claim — and articulate what additional evidence would be needed to move from one to the other.
  • 5 Recognize confounding: identify a scenario where a third variable distorts an apparent relationship between an exposure and an outcome.
  • 6 Recognize effect modification: identify when the effect of one variable on an outcome differs depending on the level of another variable.
  • 7 Apply stratification as a technique for examining relationships within subgroups and interpreting what the stratified results reveal.
  • 8 Reason counterfactually: articulate what would have needed to be different for an observed outcome to change — and why that question is central to causal inference.
Central Tendency & Variability Primary
  • 9 Define mean, median, standard deviation, and interquartile range, and calculate each from a simple dataset.
  • 10 Select the least-biased measure of central tendency for a given distribution — and explain when the mean misleads.
  • 11 Interpret a wide standard deviation as a signal that the mean may describe no individual in the group accurately — and draw appropriate conclusions.
Relative Risk & Proportional Comparison Introduced
  • 12 Interpret survival proportions as risk estimates and compare them across groups — the logic that underlies relative risk, before the formula.
  • 13 Recognize that a ratio of risks depends on which groups are compared and which are held constant — anticipating the issue of reference categories covered in the statistics session.
Study Design Named in debrief only
  • 14 Understand that formal study designs — cohort, case-control, and randomized trial — are systematic answers to the questions you asked while exploring this data. Definitions and trade-offs are covered in subsequent sessions.
How these are assessed
The Socratic tutor dialogue and debrief serve as the formative assessment for this session. Your debrief is logged by student ID and counts toward participation credit. Summative assessment of confounding, effect modification, and relative risk occurs in the Foundations of Medicine review and subsequent block sessions.
